Model Reference Adaptive Control for Spacecraft Attitude Tracking

Fixed-gain controllers assume the spacecraft you designed is the spacecraft you fly. MRAC doesn't need that assumption.

The challenge

A spacecraft's inertia matrix changes in flight: fuel burns off, solar panels extend, appendages flex. PID and LQR controllers are tuned to a nominal model and lose stability when reality drifts from it. For high-precision pointing missions (Earth observation, deep-space comms), that drift isn't acceptable. The controller has to work even when the model is wrong.

What I did

Synthesized an MRAC control law combined with a Variable Structure Controller, using time-varying adaptive gains derived from Lyapunov stability analysis. The adaptation laws guarantee bounded errors and asymptotic convergence regardless of inertia uncertainty, not just in the nominal case. Implemented in MATLAB and benchmarked head-to-head against tuned PID and LQR under two scenarios: nominal and a 3× inertia mismatch.

How performance was evaluated

Four metrics tracked across both scenarios: settling time (10 arcsecond bound), max overshoot, steady-state error, and control effort. Under nominal conditions all three controllers worked. Under large inertia uncertainty, PID and LQR both diverged into sustained oscillations and never recovered within the simulation window. MRAC adapted in real time and maintained control throughout.
